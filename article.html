DSCSA Aggregation Is Not Optional Anymore. Here Is the Checklist.

Jun 2026 · 7 min read

What Changed

For years, aggregation lived in the "eventually" column of DSCSA planning. The stabilization period let supply chains trade serialized product without every parent-child relationship documented, and plenty of programs shipped on that grace. That period is over. Trading partners now expect complete, accurate aggregation data with every serialized shipment, and wholesalers are rejecting product that arrives without it.

For regulated brands, the practical consequence is simple: aggregation is no longer a serialization feature. It is a shipping requirement. A pallet that cannot explain its own contents electronically does not move.

Aggregation, Defined

Four terms carry most of the confusion in aggregation conversations. Here is what each one actually means on a packaging line.

Serialization
Applying a unique identifier to every saleable unit: a 2D barcode carrying GTIN, serial number, lot and expiry.
Aggregation
Recording the parent-child relationships between those units and the cases and pallets that contain them, so a case code answers for every unit inside it.
Inference
A trading partner accepting a case without opening it, trusting the aggregation data to know what is inside. Inference only works when aggregation is accurate.
EPCIS
The data standard used to exchange serialization and aggregation events between trading partners.

The Checklist

Run this against your current program. Every item is a question an auditor, a wholesaler or a 3PL will eventually ask.

The DSCSA Aggregation Checklist

  1. Unit-level codes verified, not just applied. Every serialized code is camera-read after printing, and failed reads are rejected in line.
  2. Aggregation happens where packing happens. Parent-child data is captured at the moment units enter cases, not reconstructed afterward.
  3. Case and pallet labels carry their own serialized identifiers linked to their contents.
  4. EPCIS events generated for every commissioning and aggregation step, in a format your partners have actually tested.
  5. Rework has a procedure. When a case is opened and repacked, the aggregation record is updated, not orphaned.
  6. One party owns the audit trail. Batch records, verification logs and EPCIS files live in one system, retrievable in hours.
  7. Exception handling is documented. Damaged units, samples and retains are decommissioned in the data, not just removed from the line.
  8. Your printer and your packager agree on the spec. Code placement, grading targets and data formats are one document, not two interpretations.

Where Programs Fail

Codes applied but never verified

A printed code is not a working code. Programs that sample-check serialized codes discover grading failures at the wholesaler, where the cost of the discovery is a rejected shipment. 100% in-line vision verification catches the same failure while the unit is still on the line.

Aggregation at the packager, serialization at the printer

When unit codes come from one vendor and case-level aggregation from another, every handoff is a place for parent-child data to drift. The seams between vendors, not the vendors themselves, produce most aggregation errors. This is the core argument for consolidating serialized programs under one roof.

Documentation scattered across systems

The data exists, somewhere: verification logs with the printer, EPCIS files with the serialization provider, batch records with the packager. An audit that should take a morning takes two weeks of assembly.

What Good Looks Like

The programs that pass audits without drama share one design decision: serialization, verification and aggregation happen on the same line, owned by the same quality system. With EasyCode, unit-level codes are printed, camera-verified and aggregated as cases pack, and the EPCIS record leaves the building with the product. There is no reconciliation step because nothing was ever separated.

That is also why aggregation readiness is a supplier-selection question, not just an internal one. A packaging partner that treats serialization as a quality-system obligation removes the seams where aggregation fails.

Key Takeaways

  • Aggregation is now a shipping requirement: wholesalers reject serialized product that arrives without parent-child data.
  • Verify every code in line; sampled checks discover failures after the product has shipped.
  • Capture aggregation where packing happens, and keep serialization and aggregation under one owner.
  • Keep one audit trail: batch records, verification logs and EPCIS events in one retrievable system.
  • Vet your packaging partner's aggregation capability the way you vet their certifications.
