Serialization and Vendor Consolidation

Consolidating Five Vendors Into One Serialized Program

May 2026 · 9 min read
NATIONAL RX BRAND

Overview

A national prescription brand was sourcing its packaging from five suppliers: one for labels, one for cartons, one for inserts and outserts, one for serialization services and one for warehousing. Each vendor held its own spec, ran its own quality system and produced its own documentation.

Over eighteen months, the brand consolidated the entire program with Platinum Press. This case study covers what the fragmented program cost them, how the transition was phased and what the consolidated program looks like today.

The Program Before Consolidation

On paper, five specialists looked like five kinds of expertise. In practice, the seams between vendors were where the risk lived.

Five Vendors, Five Specs

Each supplier held a slightly different version of the artwork and dieline. Color targets drifted between the label stock and the carton board because no single party owned the brand standard across formats.

No Single Audit Trail

When an auditor asked for batch documentation, the quality team assembled records from five systems with five formats. Preparing for a routine audit took the better part of two weeks.

Serialization at the Seams

Unit-level codes were applied by one vendor and verified by another. Aggregation errors surfaced only at the packager, where a rejected lot meant restarting the chain across three companies.

"

The risk was never inside any one vendor. It was in the handoffs between them. Consolidation removes the handoffs.

Quality SystemsPlatinum Press, Fort Worth TX

Why Platinum Press

The brand's selection committee vetted compliance first: cGMP operations, ISO 9001:2015, G7 Master color and SGS&Co certification, verified by 100% in-line vision inspection. The deciding factor was that every format they were buying, labels, cartons, literature, lidding and security features, runs under one roof and one quality system at Platinum Press.

The Phased Rollout

Nothing was switched off until its replacement was validated. The transition ran in three phases: labels first, cartons and literature second, serialization and warehousing last.

Each phase followed the same pattern: spec re-qualification, proof approval, a parallel production run against the incumbent, then cutover. The brand's launch calendar never moved.

"

A parallel run before every cutover meant the client never had to trust a promise. They compared finished goods side by side.

Serialization, Rebuilt in Line

With EasyCode, unit-level coding, aggregation and DSCSA reporting moved onto the same line that prints and finishes the packaging. Every serialized code is camera-verified by 100% in-line inspection, so a bad code is rejected in the plant, not discovered at the packager.

Outcomes

Twelve months after full cutover, the program's numbers tell the story better than any pitch could.

5→1Vendors consolidated into a single accountable partner
100%Units camera-verified in line, including every serialized code
2 DaysAudit preparation, down from two weeks of multi-vendor assembly

Lead times settled at 3 to 4 weeks from approved artwork, with finished goods shipping from cGMP warehousing to 90% of the US in two days.

What It Means for Your Program

If your packaging program spans multiple vendors, the consolidation playbook is repeatable: vet the quality system first, phase the transition format by format and never cut over without a parallel run. Talk to a packaging expert about what that looks like for your product.
